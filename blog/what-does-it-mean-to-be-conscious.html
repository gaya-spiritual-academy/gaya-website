What Does It Mean to Be Conscious?

When Patterns Repeat, Perhaps Life Is Trying to Show You Something

Dr. Bonshuwa Chua
· June 22, 2026 · 9 min read · Malaysia · Singapore · Asia Pacific

You Thought It Was Behind You — Yet Here It Is Again

Over the years, I have noticed that many people carry a similar question, even if they express it in different ways.

Why does the same thing keep happening?

Perhaps it appears in relationships. You tell yourself that this time will be different. You choose differently, communicate differently, and promise yourself that you will not repeat the same mistakes. Yet somehow, after enough time has passed, you find yourself standing in front of a familiar feeling again.

Perhaps it appears in your work. You change roles, change companies, start a business, or move in a completely different direction. The environment changes, the people change, but the pressure, self-doubt, frustration, or need to prove yourself feels strangely familiar.

Or perhaps it appears in the things you pursue. More success. More recognition. More certainty. More security. You reach a goal that once felt important, only to discover that the satisfaction does not last as long as you imagined. Before long, another goal appears on the horizon, and the search quietly begins again.

For many people, these repeating experiences become a source of exhaustion. Not because life is difficult, but because the same inner experience seems to keep returning, wearing different faces and arriving through different circumstances.

What often hurts the most is not the repetition itself. It is what we begin to believe about ourselves because of the repetition.

We wonder if we have failed. We wonder if we have not learned enough. We wonder why we are still dealing with something we thought we had already moved beyond.

Slowly, frustration becomes self-judgment. We stop looking at the pattern and start blaming ourselves for having it.

Yet the older I become, the more I find myself asking a different question.

"What if these repeating experiences are not signs that something is wrong with us — but invitations to see something we have not yet seen?"

It Is Not a Punishment — It Is a Reminder

Most of us have been conditioned to believe that if something keeps returning, it must mean we are doing something wrong.

If the same fear comes back, we assume we have not healed. If the same challenge appears again, we assume we have not grown. If the same emotional reaction surfaces, we wonder why we are still stuck.

But perhaps repetition is not evidence of failure.

Perhaps repetition is simply life being patient.

Imagine receiving the same message again and again. The repeated message is not the problem. The repeated message is simply a sign that something remains unread.

Life often communicates in a similar way.

The experiences that continue to appear are rarely random. More often, they point toward something within us that has not yet been fully recognised. Not because we are broken, and not because we need to become someone else, but because there is still something waiting to be seen.

What makes repetition exhausting is usually not the pattern itself.

What makes it exhausting is that, without consciousness, we live entirely inside the pattern.

When we are caught in fear, we simply experience fear. When we are caught in attachment, we simply experience attachment. When we are caught in a familiar story, we become so immersed in it that we cannot see the story itself.

We only experience the emotions, the reactions, and the consequences. And because we cannot see the pattern, we continue moving within it.

This is why many people spend years trying to change their circumstances, while the same inner experience quietly follows them wherever they go.

Not because life is against them. But because life is still waiting for them to see something.

The moment we begin to see, the experience changes. The situation may remain the same, but our relationship with it becomes different.

Instead of saying, "Why is this happening to me again?", we begin asking, "What is this trying to show me?"

That small shift marks the beginning of consciousness.

It Is Not About Becoming Better — It Is About Seeing

When people hear the phrase "being conscious," they often imagine becoming calmer, wiser, more spiritual, or somehow free from difficult emotions. Yet that has never been my experience.

Fear still appears. Uncertainty still appears. Disappointment still appears. Life continues to bring situations that challenge us, stretch us, and invite us into unfamiliar territory. Being conscious does not mean these experiences disappear. It means that while they are happening, we begin to see them.

We see the fear while fear is present. We see the attachment while attachment is active. We see ourselves reaching for control, seeking approval, or reacting from old habits. For the first time, we are not only living the experience; we are aware that the experience is happening.

To me, this is one of the simplest ways to understand consciousness.

Imagine standing outside during heavy rain. The storm is intense, and all your attention is on the discomfort. You want the rain to stop. You resist it, fight it, or try to escape it.

Then something changes.

You step under shelter and look out through a window.

The rain is still falling. The storm has not disappeared. Nothing outside has changed.

Yet something inside has.

For the first time, you can see the rain.

And because you can see it, you realise you are no longer completely trapped inside it.

"This is what consciousness feels like. Not the absence of the storm, but the appearance of an opening within the storm."

The fear may still be there. The attachment may still be there. The uncertainty may still be there. Yet something new becomes available: the ability to see what is happening while it is happening.

And when that seeing appears, an exit quietly appears with it.

Not an exit from life. Not an exit from emotion. But an exit from unconscious identification.

You begin to realise that you are not the fear. You are not the attachment. You are not the story. You are the one who can see it.

This is where choice begins.

Without that opening, our reactions are automatic. We become whatever arises within us. But when that opening appears, we gain the possibility of responding rather than merely reacting.

This is why consciousness is not simply an idea or a philosophy. It is a lived experience. It is the moment we stop being completely lost in the experience and begin seeing the experience itself.

It Is Not the End of the Pattern — It Is the Beginning of Choice

One of the reasons consciousness matters is because what remains unseen tends to repeat.

As long as we are unconscious of our patterns, the future often becomes an extension of the past. The circumstances may change, but the underlying experience remains familiar. The same fears find new situations. The same attachments find new people. The same struggles appear in different forms.

From the outside, life may look different. Inside, it can feel surprisingly similar.

This is why so many people feel as though they are moving forward while somehow standing still. Not because they lack intelligence. Not because they lack effort. But because the pattern shaping their experience remains invisible.

Interestingly, the first thing that changes when consciousness appears is not necessarily the pattern itself.

  • The fear may still be there.
  • The attachment may still be there.
  • The situation may even continue to repeat.

But your relationship with it begins to change. And often, that change becomes the beginning of every other change that follows.

Because the moment you can see a pattern, you are no longer completely trapped inside it.

A new position becomes available. A place from which you can pause before reacting. A place from which you can question assumptions you once accepted as truth. A place from which you can choose something different.

Perhaps this is why consciousness is much more than a personal healing topic.

It is the beginning of every meaningful creation. Every meaningful relationship. Every meaningful decision.

Because we cannot create a different future from a place we have never consciously entered.

And when a person begins living from this space of seeing, it naturally influences the way they love, the way they choose, the way they relate, and eventually, the way they lead.

That, however, is a conversation we will explore further in the next article.

A Few Questions to Sit With

Is there a situation in your life that carries the feeling of, "Here we go again"?

If you looked at that situation without judging yourself, what might it be trying to show you?

What would happen if, instead of reacting immediately, you paused for a few seconds and simply noticed what was happening inside you?

Is there a pattern you have been trying to fix that may first be asking to be seen?

There is no need to rush toward an answer. Sometimes the beginning is not understanding. Sometimes the beginning is simply noticing.

A Gentle Reflection

Being conscious is not a battle against yourself. It is not about defeating fear, forcing yourself to let go, or becoming a different person overnight.

More often, it begins with something much gentler — moving from not seeing to seeing. From being completely immersed in an experience to recognising that the experience is happening.

很多人都会有这样的经验：换了关系、换了工作、换了环境，甚至完成了自己曾经很想达成的目标，却发现某些熟悉的纠结、抓紧、恐惧与不安依然不断出现。久而久之，我们开始怀疑自己，以为是自己不够努力、不够成熟，或哪里出了问题。

然而，生命中的重复未必是一种惩罚。它更像是一种提醒。当某些经验持续出现时，真正让人疲惫的往往不是事件本身，而是我们始终活在模式里面，却没有看见模式本身。于是我们不断重复，却不知道自己正在重复什么。

所谓「有意识」，并不是从此没有情绪，也不是立刻改变自己。有意识，是当事情发生的时候，你开始能够看见自己正在经历什么。当这个看见出现时，一个出口也同时出现了——不是逃离生命，而是不再被无意识的反应所困住。从这里开始，我们拥有了选择，也拥有了创造不同未来的可能。
